\section{Research goals}\label{sec:goal}

The goal of this research is to establish best practices for modeling large railway systems in answer set programming.
Our initial goal has been to understand large railway systems by leveraging Flatland as a virtual railway environment generator and simulator.
In doing so, developing a comprehensive formalization of the problem, 
as well as initial encodings, 
has enabled us to see how the problems in modeling railway systems 
align with or differ from those encountered in similar domains.

Following this, the aim is to exhaustively complete the Flatland problem,
which necessitates a solution to both the 
vehicle scheduling problem (VSP) and
the vehicle rescheduling problem (VRSP).
Described in more detail in Section~\ref{sec:issues},
the extension into VRSP requires the ability to handle
stochastic malfunctions,
which represent breakdowns and delays that occur every day
in real railway networks.
VSP instances take place during the operational planning phase
and since these occur ahead of time,
we can be more forgiving about how long it takes to find an acceptable solution.
VRSP instances take place in response to unforeseen events,
and since the surrounding conditions can change in real time,
preference is given to finding a solution quickly
over finding an optimal solution.

Beyond just Flatland, this body of understanding can be translated to more general problems faced by railway operators.
Narayanaswami and Rangaraj classify problems in railway operations into broad categories from planning to execution \cite{narran2011a}.
Specific problems from across that spectrum that are worth addressing include
\begin{itemize}
	\item routing and scheduling
	\item preemptive replanning for adverse events
	\item valid switch position logic (such as that used by the ILTIS system)
	\item energy utilization
	\item route synchronization
	\item modeling speed and acceleration
	\item personnel management
	\item rolling stock management
	\item maintenance scheduling
\end{itemize}
Although solutions to many issues in the realm of transportation are rooted in public policy,
it is my hope that this research can contribute to the solving of problems that require or can benefit from technological solutions.
