\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Research into railway operations began as early as the 1950s and 1960s, when such papers as \textit{A Model for the Optimal Programming of Railway Freight Train Movements} in 1956 and \textit{Timetables for a Suburban Rail Transit System} in 1969 sought to formalize problems in the domain by appealing to fields such as management science and linear programming.
	\item For the rest of the twentieth century, the railway operations literature grew as researchers explored solutions involving dynamic programming and branch and bound procedures (cite: Petersen/Merchant), integer programming and heuristics (cite Cai and Goh), Lagrangian relaxation (cite: Lindberg,  Nou, Nilsson), among others
	\item Answer set programming has tackled problems in similar domains, such as multi-agent pathfinding, job shop scheduling, airline and traffic management, etc.
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item The hybrid railway scheduling paper for SBB (cite and expand)
		\item Some of Klaus's papers for MAPF
		\item Martin Gebser's Decomposition strategies
		\item Alice's Traffic Signal paper from TAASP
		\item Alexander's Air Traffic Flow work on capacity
	\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
