One of the most interesting developments has been gaining
a better understanding of how the Flatland problem differs from other
well-explored domains, for instance multi-agent pathfinding.
In common multi-agent pathfinding problems,
agents exist in a grid and have a consistent number of degrees of freedom,
meaning aside from the presence of obstacles or boundaries,
agents are free to move to any unoccupied adjacent cell.
In Flatland, movements become more restricted,
for example because trains may only move forward,
meaning we have to preserve information about their orientation.
Furthermore, trains are bounded to the topology of the track,
limiting their possible transitions to only one or two adjacent cells,
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tracks}.

To take advantage of these more narrowly-defined conditions,
the aforementioned \textit{Flaspland} paper puts forth a hypothesis that 
alternative representations of the environment yield performance advantages
over the standard grid-based environment as the problem scales.
We first developed encodings that translate the environment into various graph structures,
and then use a common pathfinding encoding,
whose rules correspond to those defined in our formalization,
to calculate collision-free paths
along each of the graphs.
To supply a baseline for comparison, 
we retrofitted an existing multi-agent pathfinding encoding \cite{!!}
that takes as input the standard grid-based environment.
The results show that as the number of agents in an environment grows,
the alternative representations outperform the baseline \cite{munerosc26a}.
While the alternative approaches tend to fall behind in grounding time,
they make up for it in solving time.
This suggests that there may yet be potential improvements if grounding
can be further reduced.
From these initial results, we are excited to explore
different styles of encodings,
further alternative representations of environments,
and difference logic,
to name a few.
